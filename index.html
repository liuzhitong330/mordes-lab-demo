When One Kinase Falls:
TBK1 as the Master Conductor of Neuronal Autophagy in ALS

Interactive demo · Mordes Lab, UCSF · Data: Smeyers & Mordes, Cell Reports 2025 + Allen Brain Atlas ISH

Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis is defined by one paradox: motor neurons — among the largest, most metabolically active cells in the body — are selectively destroyed, while neighboring cell types survive. One clue lies in the gene TBK1. Haploinsufficiency of TBK1 causes familial ALS, and TBK1 variants are found in sporadic cases too. But why does losing half of one kinase kill a motor neuron?

The Mordes lab recently answered this with unusual precision. Using deep phosphoproteomics in iPSC-derived excitatory neurons — quantifying 66,278 phosphopeptides across 8,196 proteins — they showed that TBK1 doesn't target a single pathway. It phosphorylates a coordinated network: five selective autophagy cargo receptors (SQSTM1/p62, OPTN, NDP52, NBR1, TAX1BP1), two autophagy initiation factors (FIP200, ATG9A), and the late-endosomal GTPase RAB7A. Lose TBK1, and all of these collapse simultaneously.

Figure 1 — The TBK1 Phosphorylation Network

Hover nodes for phosphosite and function · Data: Smeyers & Mordes, Cell Reports 2025 (ProteomeXchange PXD068150)

The network reveals a striking architecture: TBK1 operates on three distinct layers of autophagy simultaneously. At the recognition layer, it phosphorylates all five major cargo receptors — ensuring that misfolded proteins and damaged organelles get flagged. At the initiation layer, TBK1 phosphorylates FIP200 (pS1286) and ATG9A (pS755), the proteins that nucleate the autophagosome membrane. And at the trafficking layer, it phosphorylates RAB7A (pS72) to keep late endosomes moving toward lysosomes. Losing TBK1 is not a partial impairment — it is a simultaneous collapse of recognition, initiation, and delivery.

Figure 2 — Regional Expression: Why Motor Neurons?

The phosphoproteomics tells us what TBK1 does. The Allen Mouse Brain Atlas tells us where it matters most. If motor neurons are uniquely vulnerable to TBK1 loss, we would expect TBK1 and its key effectors to be highest in motor-circuit regions — and lowest in regions spared by ALS.

The pattern is unambiguous. Motor cortex (MOp) has the highest TBK1 expression of any region (0.039), and also the highest ATG9A (0.039) — the two autophagy nodes most co-enriched in motor tissue. Cerebellum, the region largely spared in ALS, has the lowest expression of both (0.009 each). FIP200/Rb1cc1 follows the same gradient: MOp > Hipp > Cereb.

This is not a coincidence of proximity — it is a dependency signature. Motor neurons run TBK1-dependent autophagy at the highest throughput of any region examined. When a TBK1 mutation cuts that flux in half, it is precisely the cells with the highest autophagic demand that cannot compensate. SQSTM1 (p62) is broadly expressed (0.10–0.18 across regions), consistent with its role as a universal cargo receptor; but its phosphorylation at S361/S366 — and therefore its function — depends entirely on TBK1 activity that is highest in MOp.

An open question the Mordes lab is positioned to answer: does the ratio of TBK1 to p62 predict regional vulnerability? MOp has relatively lower p62 (0.16) but very high TBK1 (0.039) — suggesting the cargo receptor is not the bottleneck, but the kinase is. Regions with the same p62 level but lower TBK1 (Str: p62 0.146, TBK1 0.014) may clear cargo through TBK1-independent mechanisms, explaining their sparing.
