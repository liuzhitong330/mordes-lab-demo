When Mitophagy Fails:
TBK1 Loss Triggers a Mitochondrial Cascade and Synaptic Collapse in Human Neurons

Interactive demo · Mordes Lab, UCSF · Data: GEO GSE308008 (Smeyers & Mordes, Cell Reports 2025) + Allen Brain Atlas ISH

Why do motor neurons die in ALS? One answer lies in TBK1, a kinase whose haploinsufficiency is sufficient to cause familial ALS and FTD. The Mordes lab recently mapped TBK1's phosphorylation targets in human iPSC-derived neurons with unprecedented depth — 66,278 phosphopeptides — revealing that TBK1 coordinates five cargo receptors, two autophagy initiation factors, and the late-endosomal GTPase RAB7A simultaneously. Lose TBK1, and all of these collapse at once.

But the phosphoproteomics tells us what TBK1 phosphorylates, not what neurons do in response. The companion RNA-seq dataset (GEO GSE308008, 24 samples across six isogenic genotypes) reveals the transcriptional aftermath — and it points to a cascade that begins with mitochondria and ends at the synapse.

Figure 1 — The Transcriptional Response to TBK1 Loss in Human Neurons

Each dot below is a gene expressed in iPSC-derived excitatory neurons. The x-axis shows its log₂ fold-change in complete TBK1 knockout (KO); the y-axis shows the same in E696K homozygous mutant neurons (the ALS-causing mutation). Genes that move the same way in both conditions lie near the diagonal. Genes that diverge — moving in KO but not the mutation — reveal processes selectively sensitive to total TBK1 loss.

Mito = mitochondria-encoded transcripts; Autophagy = lysosomal/selective autophagy genes; Synapse = presynaptic vesicle and receptor genes; STING = innate immune/cGAS-STING pathway; ALS = ALS-linked genes.

The most striking pattern: every mitochondria-encoded transcript is substantially upregulated in both TBK1 KO and E696K mutant neurons (top-right cluster). MT-CO1, MT-CO2, MT-CYB, MT-ND1-5, and multiple mitochondrial tRNAs all rise 0.5–1.1 log₂FC. At the same time, the nuclear-encoded mitochondrial import protein TOMM20 falls (−0.37), and the mitophagy receptors BNIP3L (−0.29) and BNIP3 (−0.19) decrease. Together this signals accumulation of damaged mitochondria that can no longer be cleared.

One consequence: when mitophagy fails and damaged mitochondria accumulate, mitochondrial DNA and RNA leak into the cytoplasm — a pattern known to activate the cGAS-STING innate immune pathway. In our data, TMEM173 (STING) is among the most upregulated non-mitochondrial genes in TBK1 KO neurons (+0.89 log₂FC), with a smaller but consistent rise in E696K mutants (+0.73). This may explain why TBK1 loss does not merely impair autophagy — it triggers a neuroimmune response within the neuron itself.

Meanwhile, synaptic vesicle genes collapse (bottom-left). SV2B falls −1.14 log₂FC, SNAP25 −0.50, SV2A −0.48, GRIA1 (AMPA receptor subunit) −0.48. The ALS biomarker STMN2 — whose cryptic splicing is a hallmark of TDP-43 pathology — is mildly reduced (−0.11), consistent with early proteostatic stress in these neurons.

Sv2b = synaptic vesicle glycoprotein 2B (most downregulated synapse gene); Snap25 = presynaptic SNARE; Stmn2 = ALS biomarker stathmin-2; Bnip3l = mitophagy receptor NIX; Tmem173 = STING.

The pattern is striking: Sv2b is highest in motor cortex (MOp: 0.147) and somatosensory cortex (SSp: 0.139), and lowest in cerebellum (0.020) — precisely tracking ALS vulnerability versus sparing. Motor neurons that most depend on SV2B-mediated synaptic vesicle cycling are the same cells that lose SV2B transcription when TBK1 fails. Similarly, SNAP25 is enriched in MOp (0.188) and PFC (0.199). STMN2 peaks in MOp (0.153), consistent with its established role as a motor axon health marker.

BNIP3L (the mitophagy receptor NIX, downregulated in TBK1 KO) is moderately expressed throughout (EC: 0.038, MOp: 0.023), suggesting that mitophagy dependence is widespread — but its loss in high-demand motor neurons may be especially consequential. An open question: are TBK1 haploinsufficient motor neurons already running at the edge of their mitophagy capacity, such that a 50% reduction in kinase activity crosses a threshold that neighboring cell types can tolerate?
